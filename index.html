What to send

This page does not have a live upload system yet, so submissions go by email and get added by hand. Send the items below to a.helgerson@ufl.edu and it will be reviewed and posted to the site.
  1. Your name, major, and year: So the project can be credited (this can include advisors).
  2. Project title: A short name for what you observed or built.
  3. Observation date: When the data or images were captured.
  4. Description: What you observed, heliostat settings used, conditions, and anything notable about the session.
  5. Images and/or data files: Attach directly to the email. Original resolution images work best.

Solar Features

What to look for when observing the Sun. Each feature below is visible in specific SDO AIA wavelengths or in white light. Images are pulled live from NASA SDO.

Prominences
Best seen: 304 Å (He II)
Large, bright, dense structures of plasma suspended in the corona above the solar limb, anchored to the surface by magnetic fields. Prominences can last for weeks before destabilizing. When seen against the solar disk rather than the limb they appear dark and are called filaments.
Chromosphere Magnetic Long-lived
Filaments
Best seen: 304 Å and 171 Å
The same structures as prominences, but viewed against the solar disk. They appear as dark, elongated ribbons threading across the chromosphere. Filaments follow magnetic polarity inversion lines (PILs) and are excellent tracers of subsurface magnetic complexity. A disappearing filament (filament eruption) often precedes a coronal mass ejection (CME).
Chromosphere CME Precursor Polarity Inversion
Sunspots
Best seen: HMI White Light (Visible)
Dark regions on the photosphere where intense magnetic field bundles suppress convection, causing localised cooling (around 3,500 K vs. the photospheric average of 5,778 K). Sunspots appear in pairs or groups of opposite magnetic polarity. Their count is a key indicator of the phase of the 11-year solar cycle. Complex spot groups (delta configurations) are most likely to produce X-class flares.
Photosphere Solar Cycle Flare Source
Solar Flares
Best seen: 193 Å and 131 Å
Sudden, intense brightenings in the solar atmosphere caused by the rapid release of magnetic energy stored in active regions. Classified A through X by their peak X-ray flux in the 1 to 8 Å band (see the GOES X-ray panel on this site). X-class flares can cause HF radio blackouts on the sunlit side of Earth. They are often accompanied by CMEs and energetic particle events.
Corona X-Ray Radio Blackout
Coronal Holes
Best seen: 193 Å and 211 Å
Large, dark regions of the corona where magnetic field lines are open and extend outward into the heliosphere rather than looping back to the surface. Fast solar wind streams (600 to 800 km/s) flow freely out of coronal holes. When a coronal hole faces Earth, the arriving high-speed stream can enhance geomagnetic activity 3 to 4 days later and drive elevated Kp indices.
Corona Open Field Fast Wind Kp Driver
Coronal Loops
Best seen: 171 Å and 211 Å
Arching structures of hot plasma (1 to 3 million K) that trace closed magnetic field lines connecting regions of opposite polarity on the photosphere. Coronal loops are the building blocks of active regions and prominences. Their brightness indicates concentrated magnetic flux. Destabilising loops can reconnect and release energy as flares or eruptive events.
Corona Closed Field Active Regions
Active Regions
Best seen: 171 Å, 193 Å, 211 Å
Concentrated areas of strong, complex magnetic field on the Sun, often surrounding sunspot groups. Active regions are the primary sites of flares, CMEs, and other energetic events. NOAA assigns numbered designations (e.g., AR 3800) and tracks them across the solar disk. Their evolution from emergence to decay governs much of the short-term space weather threat.
Photosphere to Corona NOAA Numbered Space Weather
Magnetic Field (Magnetogram)
Best seen: HMI Magnetogram
The HMI magnetogram maps the line-of-sight component of the photospheric magnetic field. White (positive polarity) and dark (negative polarity) regions show where field lines emerge and return. Closely interlaced opposite-polarity regions indicate stored magnetic energy and potential for eruption. Magnetograms are the foundation of all solar activity forecasting.
Photosphere Polarity Forecasting
Flare Ribbons
Best seen: 1600 Å (UV Continuum)
Bright, elongated brightening that appear on either side of a magnetic polarity inversion line during and after a flare. They form as energetic particles and radiation accelerated during magnetic reconnection rain down along field lines, heating the chromosphere. The separation speed and brightness of ribbons correlates with flare intensity and the rate of reconnection.
Chromosphere Reconnection UV
Note on SunPy automated detection: Automated feature detection (filament tracing, active region segmentation, coronal hole detection) can be done with SunPy and the SunPy documentation. Because SunPy is a Python library that runs server-side, it requires a backend (e.g., a Python/Flask server or Jupyter notebook) that downloads FITS files from the JSOC data archive and runs detection algorithms such as the CHIMERA coronal hole finder or SMART active region detection. If you would like this integrated, the team can set up a GitHub Action or small server script that runs SunPy nightly and outputs annotated images into the repository.

ERAU Solar Observatory

About

History, assembly, and team behind the ERAU solar telescope and heliostat system.

Instrument History

  • Origin The ERAU solar telescope program began as a student-led initiative to bring ground-based solar observation to the Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University campus in Daytona Beach, FL.
  • Assembly The heliostat system was assembled and aligned by undergraduate students in the Department of Physical Sciences. A heliostat uses a motorized flat mirror to track the Sun and feed a fixed telescope, allowing safe, stable observations throughout the day.
  • Commissioning First light was achieved following polar alignment and optical calibration. The telescope produces solar images in narrowband H-alpha as well as white light, enabling observation of sunspots, prominences, and chromospheric features.
  • This Console The web console was developed to complement ground-based observations with live space weather data from NOAA SWPC and NASA SDO, giving observers real-time context for what they see through the eyepiece.
  • Ongoing Student observers submit images and reports through the Submissions page. The program is open to all ERAU students and welcomes collaboration with faculty and visiting researchers.

The Instrument

The ERAU solar telescope uses a heliostat design: a motorized flat mirror tracks the Sun and redirects sunlight into a fixed optical path. This allows a large, heavy telescope to remain stationary while the moving mirror does the tracking work.

Observers can view the Sun in H-alpha (656.3 nm), which highlights chromospheric features like filaments, prominences, and flares. White-light viewing reveals sunspots and granulation on the photosphere.
